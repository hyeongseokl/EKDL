# Predicting U.S. Unemployment Rate Using Macroeconomic Indicators: GDP Growth, CPI, and Lagged Feature Modeling
### Contributors:
Ethan Kim
David Hyeongseok Lee
### Summary
This project analyzes how key macroeconomic indicators relate to and can predict the U.S. quarterly unemployment rate. The motivation comes from historic economic theory, which describes the inverse relationship between GDP growth and unemployment. We wanted to test how well this relationship holds using real data and whether adding inflation data through the Consumer Price Index (CPI) strengthens the predictive power of our models.
Our central research question is: can quarterly GDP growth rate and commodity CPI reliably predict the U.S. unemployment rate, and how do different modeling approaches compare in doing so? To answer this, we collected three publicly available datasets from FRED (Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis): the U.S. Real GDP Growth Rate, the U.S. Unemployment Rate, and the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers: Commodities. After standardizing all three to a quarterly format and merging them on the date column, we performed exploratory analysis and built a series of predictive models.
From our models we predicted the historical mean unemployment rate of 5.79% for all quarters, producing an RMSE of 1.7996 percentage points. Any model we build needs to beat this to demonstrate real predictive value. We trimmed our dataset to 1990 and onward to focus on the recent world, removing pre 1990 data that reflected structurally different conditions. We used a strict chronological train/test split, training on 1990 Q1 through 2019 Q4 and testing on 2021 Q1 through 2025 Q4, since randomly splitting time series data would mean data leakage. The COVID-19 pandemic caused extreme outlier quarters in Q2 and Q3 of 2020 that were removed from the dataset entirely. With only two affected quarters out of roughly 260 observations, using a dummy variable was not feasible for training and generalization.
Our initial linear regression using raw features performed worse than baseline with an RMSE of 2.66, which we traced to CPI's long-term upward trend dominating predictions. After first differencing CPI to remove this trend, performance improved substantially. We then engineered lagged features at 1, 2, and 4 quarter intervals for GDP growth rate, differenced CPI, capturing the delayed effects that macroeconomic conditions have on labor markets.
With these features we evaluated four models against the baseline. Ridge Regression emerged as the best performer with an RMSE of 0.3307 and R^2 of 0.7977, outperforming Linear Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost. Ridge's strong performance over tree-based models suggests the relationship between these macroeconomic indicators and unemployment is largely linear, and that regularization effectively handles the multicollinearity introduced by lagged features on a relatively small dataset of roughly 140 training quarters. Using only three publicly available FRED variables, our best model predicts quarterly unemployment within approximately 0.33 percentage points and explains roughly 80% of the variance in the test period.

### Data Profile:
Dataset 1: U.S. Real GDP Growth Rate
Source: U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis, published via FRED. URL: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/A191RL1Q225SBEA. Format: CSV, quarterly time series. Temporal coverage: Q1 1947 to present. File location in repository: data/raw/gdp_growth.csv.
This dataset contains the quarterly percent change in real U.S. GDP, seasonally adjusted at annual rates. It is the primary economic growth indicator in our project and serves as a core predictor variable. Each row represents one quarter, first date of that quarter. The values range from strongly negative during recessions to strongly positive during recovery periods, which is reflected in the wide standard deviation across our merged dataset.
This data originates from a U.S. federal government agency and is published through FRED. Federal government works are in the public domain and permits free use with proper attribution. The dataset contains no personally identifiable information.
Dataset 2: U.S. Unemployment Rate
Source: U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, published via FRED. URL: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/UNRATE. Format: CSV, monthly time series aggregated to quarterly. Temporal coverage: January 1948 to present. File location in repository: data/raw/unemployment_rate.csv.
This dataset contains the monthly seasonally adjusted unemployment rate, representing the percentage of the labor force that is actively seeking work but unable to find it. Because GDP is only reported quarterly, we aggregated monthly unemployment values to quarterly by averaging the three months within each quarter. This is our target variable throughout all modeling. In the merged dataset it ranges from a low of 3.4% to a high of 13.0%, with the maximum occurring during the COVID-19 shock in Q2 2020. This data is also publicly available U.S. government data published through FRED, free to use with attribution and containing no personally identifiable information.
Dataset 3: Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers: Commodities (CUSR0000SAC)
Source: U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, published via FRED. URL: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/CUSR0000SAC. Format: CSV, monthly time series aggregated to quarterly. Temporal coverage: January 1956 to present, seasonally adjusted. File location in repository: data/raw/cpi_commodities.csv.
This dataset tracks the average price levels of physical goods for U.S. urban consumers relative to a 1982 to 1984 baseline of 100. It was added after our initial project plan because we felt that GDP and unemployment alone would provide too limited a feature set for meaningful modeling. Monthly values were averaged to quarterly to align with the other datasets. The index has grown steadily from roughly 31 in 1956 to over 226 by late 2025, making it a strongly non-stationary series that required first differencing before use in regression models. As with the other datasets, this is publicly available U.S. government data with no personally identifiable information, which makes it free to use with attribution.
Integrated Dataset
The three datasets were merged on the observation_date column after standardizing all dates to quarterly ISO 8601 format (YYYY-MM-DD). An inner join was used so only quarters present across all three sources were retained. A covid_dummy binary variable was also added during exploratory analysis to flag Q2 and Q3 2020, though these rows were ultimately removed from the modeling dataset. The final integrated file is located at data/processed/joined_data.csv and covers 280 quarters from Q1 1956 through Q4 2025. The five columns are observation_date, gdp_growth_rate, unemployment_rate, cpi_commodities, and covid_dummy. GDP growth rate ranges from -28.0% to 34.9% with a mean of 3.05% and standard deviation of 4.34. Unemployment rate ranges from 3.4% to 13.0% with a mean of 5.82% and standard deviation of 1.67. CPI commodities ranges from an index value of 31.17 to 226.96 with a mean of 116.01 and standard deviation of 60.93. There are no missing values in any column. The extreme values in both GDP growth rate and unemployment rate correspond to the two COVID-19 quarters, where GDP growth swung from -28.0% to 34.9% and unemployment peaked at 13.0%. These are real observations and not data errors, but were removed from the modeling dataset for the reasons described in the Data Cleaning section.

### Data Quality
Overall, the quality of our three datasets was quite high, which is expected given that they all originate from U.S. federal government agencies and are distributed through FRED. That said, we still performed a systematic assessment before proceeding with any analysis or modeling.
The first thing we checked across all three datasets was completeness. After loading each CSV into a pandas DataFrame, we ran null checks using isnull().sum() and found zero missing values in any column across all three datasets. This is consistent with how FRED publishes data, as gaps in government economic series are typically filled through revised estimates rather than left blank.
The second concern was temporal alignment. Our three datasets did not share the same native frequency. GDP growth rate is reported quarterly, which made it our anchor. Unemployment rate and CPI commodities are both reported monthly, so they required aggregation before merging was possible. This difference in reporting frequency was a known constraint from the start of the project and was addressed during the cleaning process described in the next section.
After merging, the integrated dataset contains 280 quarterly observations spanning Q1 1956 through Q4 2025 with no missing values in any column. The date column has exactly 280 unique values with no duplicates, confirming the merge produced a clean one-to-one match across all quarters.
We also assessed the statistical properties of each variable. GDP growth rate has a mean of 3.05% and a standard deviation of 4.34, with a minimum of -28.0% and a maximum of 34.9%. The unemployment rate has a mean of 5.82% and a standard deviation of 1.67, ranging from 3.4% to 13.0%. CPI commodities range from 31.17 to 226.96 with a mean of 116.01 and a standard deviation of 60.93. The extreme values in GDP growth and unemployment are not errors. They correspond to Q2 and Q3 of 2020, when the COVID-19 pandemic caused a historic economic contraction followed by a sharp rebound. These were confirmed as legitimate data points and handled deliberately rather than discarded as noise.
A more meaningful quality concern we identified was stationarity. CPI commodities show a strong and consistent long term upward trend across the entire dataset, growing from roughly 31 in 1956 to over 226 by 2025. This means the series is non-stationary, which violates assumptions underlying linear regression and many time series models. Left uncorrected, this caused our first regression model to produce worse than baseline results because CPI dominated the predictions with its trend. The unemployment rate also shows mild non-stationarity over certain periods. GDP growth rate, already expressed as a percent change, is roughly stationary. These stationarity issues were addressed through first differencing during the cleaning stage.
Finally, we noted that the correlation between our variables is relatively weak. GDP growth and unemployment have a correlation of -0.084, which is directionally consistent with Okun's Law but weaker than economic theory might suggest at this level of aggregation. GDP growth and CPI have a correlation of -0.12, and unemployment and CPI are nearly uncorrelated at -0.0024. These low correlations are not data quality issues per se, but they do signal that simple linear relationships between these variables may have limited predictive power, which informed our decision to also explore nonlinear models.
### Data Cleaning
Our raw datasets required relatively minimal cleaning given their high quality source, but several important operations were performed to make them suitable for analysis and modeling.
The first operation was aggregating the monthly datasets to quarterly frequency. Both the unemployment rate and CPI commodities datasets are published monthly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, while GDP growth is only available quarterly. To align all three, we averaged the three monthly values within each quarter for both unemployment and CPI. For example, the Q1 unemployment rate was computed as the average of January, February, and March values. This ensured that our quarterly CPI and unemployment figures were representative of the full quarter rather than just a single month snapshot.
The second operation was standardizing the date column across all three datasets to ISO 8601 format (YYYY-MM-DD) and ensuring each date represented the first day of the quarter. This was necessary because the raw FRED files use slightly different date string formats, and consistent formatting was required for the merge to work correctly.
The third operation was merging the three cleaned datasets into a single integrated file using an inner join on the observation_date column through pandas. The inner join meant only quarters present in all three datasets were retained, which naturally resolved any edge cases where one dataset started earlier or ended later than another. The resulting merged dataset has 280 rows with no gaps.
The fourth operation was renaming the raw FRED column names to human readable labels. The original column names A191RL1Q225SBEA, UNRATE, and CUSR0000SAC were renamed to gdp_growth_rate, unemployment_rate, and cpi_commodities respectively.
The fifth operation was handling the COVID-19 outlier quarters. Q2 and Q3 of 2020 produced extreme values across all three variables due to the pandemic. We initially added a covid_dummy binary variable to flag these quarters so models could account for them. However, with only two affected quarters out of 280 observations, training a model on this dummy variable was not feasible. A model trained on non-COVID data would have no meaningful way to generalize predictions for COVID conditions and vice versa. We therefore removed these two rows from the modeling dataset entirely.
The sixth operation was first differencing the CPI commodities series and the unemployment rate series prior to modeling. Because CPI has a strong upward trend spanning nearly 70 years, using it in its raw form caused it to dominate regression predictions. First differencing transforms the series into quarter-over-quarter changes, which are stationary and more appropriate for regression and time series modeling. The same was applied to the unemployment rate to improve model specification.
### Findings
Our goal from the start of this project was to determine whether publicly available macroeconomic indicators could reliably predict the U.S. quarterly unemployment rate. After cleaning and preparing our data, we trained and evaluated four models on quarterly data from 1990 through 2019 and tested them on the post-COVID period from 2021 through 2025. The features used were GDP growth rate, first-differenced CPI commodities, and lagged versions of all three variables at 1, 2, and 4 quarters back.
We started with a mean baseline model that simply predicts the historical average unemployment rate for every quarter. This produced an RMSE of 1.7996 percentage points and a deeply negative R² on the test set, which reflects just how unusual the post-COVID labor market is as a test period. Any model we built needed to meaningfully beat this to demonstrate real predictive value.
All four of our trained models did exactly that. Linear Regression achieved an RMSE of 0.3713 and an R² of 0.745. Random Forest produced an RMSE of 0.3816 and an R² of 0.731. XGBoost came in at an RMSE of 0.4073 and an R² of 0.693. Ridge Regression outperformed all others with an RMSE of 0.3307 and an R² of 0.798, meaning it explained roughly 80% of the variance in unemployment over the test period using only three macroeconomic variables.
The strong performance of Ridge Regression over the tree-based models is an interesting result in itself. It suggests the relationship between these macroeconomic indicators and unemployment is largely linear in nature. Ridge does not learn complex nonlinear decision boundaries the way Random Forest and XGBoost do. What it does is apply regularization to handle the multicollinearity that naturally arises between lagged features, which is a real concern when you include 1, 2, and 4 quarter lags of the same variables. On a relatively small dataset of around 140 training quarters, that regularization appears to be exactly what was needed.
The importance of lagged features also stands out as a key finding. Including prior quarter values of GDP growth, unemployment, and CPI was critical to model performance. This directly reflects economic theory. GDP growth and price level changes do not affect unemployment instantaneously. There is a delay before businesses respond to shifting economic conditions by hiring or letting workers go. 
The decision to remove COVID quarters was also validated by our results. The Q2 and Q3 2020 observations represented a structural break, with unemployment spiking to 13% in a single quarter, a level not seen since the Great Depression. No model trained on historical patterns could reasonably predict that, and including those quarters would have distorted our error metrics without adding meaningful insight.
Overall our findings show that with just three publicly available variables from FRED and a straightforward modeling approach, it is possible to predict quarterly U.S. unemployment with an RMSE of approximately 0.33 percentage points and explain around 80% of the variance in the test period. For a small and interpretable feature set, that is a strong result.
### Future Work
One of the most immediate improvements would be expanding the feature set beyond the three variables used in this project. GDP growth rate, unemployment rate, and CPI commodities capture broad economic trends but miss many factors that influence labor market conditions. Adding variables such as the federal funds interest rate, labor force participation rate, job openings data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics JOLTS survey, or consumer confidence indices could meaningfully improve predictive power. All of these are publicly available through FRED and could be integrated using the same pipeline we already built.
A natural extension would also be exploring proper time series models such as ARIMA or ARIMAX. Our current models treat each quarter as an independent observation with manually engineered lags, whereas dedicated time series models are specifically designed to capture autocorrelation and temporal dependencies in a more principled way. A Vector Autoregression model could be particularly useful here since it would allow all three variables to influence each other simultaneously rather than treating unemployment as the sole target.
Our training data was restricted to 1990 onward to avoid older economic regimes that no longer reflect modern conditions. However, even within the post-1990 period there are distinct economic environments such as the dot-com era, the 2008 financial crisis, and the post COVID recovery. A regime-switching model that allows its parameters to change across different economic cycles could produce more accurate predictions than a single static model trained across all periods.
The test period we used, 2021 through 2025, was intentionally chosen to reflect real world conditions after the COVID structural break. While our best model achieved an R^2 of 0.80 on this period, it is worth noting this was a period of unusual labor market tightness following the pandemic. Testing on additional holdout periods such as the 2008 to 2010 recession would give a more complete picture of how well the models generalize across different economic conditions.
Our current pipeline predicts one quarter ahead. Extending to multi-step forecasting at 2, 4, or 8 quarters ahead would be more practically useful for policy and planning purposes, though prediction uncertainty naturally grows with the forecast horizon. Quantifying and communicating that uncertainty through confidence intervals rather than point estimates alone would also be a valuable addition.
From a data curation perspective, one lesson this project reinforced is the importance of checking stationarity before any modeling work begins. Our early failures with linear regression were directly caused by skipping this step, and it cost significant time to diagnose. Future work on any time series project should treat stationarity testing as a mandatory first step in the data preparation workflow rather than something to discover after models produce poor results.
Finally, the small size of our dataset, roughly 140 training quarters, was a consistent limiting factor. This constrained the effectiveness of tree-based models and limited our ability to use more complex architectures. As more quarterly data accumulates over time, retraining these models on larger datasets would be worthwhile. Alternatively, incorporating monthly data rather than aggregating to quarterly would triple the number of observations and could meaningfully improve model performance while also enabling shorter-horizon forecasting.

### Challenges
The first major challenge was our initial train/test split. We accidentally used a random split by default, which caused data leakage since the model was being trained on future data and tested on past data. This produced very poor results early on and took time to diagnose. Switching to a strict chronological split resolved the issue and is a critical requirement for any time series modeling task.
Stationarity was another significant issue. Our first linear regression produced an RMSE of 2.66, worse than the baseline. The cause was CPI commodities, which trends steadily from 31 in 1956 to over 226 by 2025 and was dominating model predictions. Applying first differencing to CPI and unemployment brought the RMSE down to 0.53 and is a necessary step when working with non-stationary economic series.
Handling the COVID-19 quarters required a deliberate methodological decision. We initially planned to use a dummy variable to account for Q2 and Q3 2020, but with only two affected quarters out of the entire dataset, a dummy variable was not trainable in any meaningful way. We removed those quarters entirely to avoid outlier contamination of our error metrics.
Dataset size was a consistent constraint throughout. After restricting to post-1990 data and removing COVID quarters, we had roughly 140 training observations. This limited the effectiveness of tree-based models like Random Forest and XGBoost, and is likely a key reason Ridge Regression, which regularizes rather than adds complexity, outperformed them.
Finally, determining the appropriate historical range for training data was not straightforward. Using data from 1956 onward introduced older economic regimes that do not reflect modern relationships between GDP, unemployment, and inflation. Restricting to 1990 forward produced more relevant and consistent training signal.
